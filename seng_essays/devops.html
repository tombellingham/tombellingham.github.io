Should software engineers handle both development and operations, or is it better to have specialist teams?

Devising the right ‘team topology’ is about balancing the distribution of knowledge and responsibility across employees to optimise the ‘full cycle’ delivery of a system. What setup is best will depend on factors like how many products and staff a company have, the strength of their technical leadership, and their willingness to embrace change.[1]

For specialised dev and ops teams, sharing relevant knowledge[2] with each other is the main challenge. Without the right knowledge, operators can take longer to diagnose and resolve problems because they don’t understand software implementation, and developers may make design choices or omit features that impact operations unwittingly. There is also an efficiency bottleneck in handing off issues3 to another team, which can be slow, especially when both teams take turns to iteratively narrow down a problem. The benefits of this setup are that developers can focus[3] more easily on their speciality and expert operators can often make better judgements[4] and operational decisions than a developer who only knows ‘enough to make it work.’

The challenges for software engineers in charge of both dev and ops are that they need to be interested in and learn knowledge and skills related to the whole tech stack, bear more responsibility, and they need to see the value of this approach and be committed3 to making it work - all these factors incur a risk of burnout due to cognitive load and stress. Additionally, there needs to be strong technical leadership, sufficient staff, ongoing training (esp. for newcomers), and great tooling to help them cover all responsibilities3. ‘Regular’ engineers may also lack the broad perspective and wisdom of expert operators4, which can impact corporate trust[5]. However, the great strength in this approach is that it becomes easier to integrate end-to-end feedback3 and therefore easier to make frequent and routine deployments; developers can quickly research issues themselves and make changes without waiting for other teams. Developers may also internalise their responsibility better since operational problems can directly impact their lives[6].

Netflix are a great example of a hybrid between these two extremes. Instead of having specialised teams of operators, they have specialised teams of developers to create reusable tooling to help each product team manage their own dev and ops3. In this way their product software engineers are put in charge of the full cycle and still supported by domain specialists. Another good strategy is to have developers spend some time working in production[7] (as Sam Newman suggested last week, “the best first job you can have is production support”).

At an investment bank where Arthur worked, developers would focus only on narrow problem solving domains. Operation would consume more time than any developer could spare. Operators would explore new issues only enough to assign them to the right people, they would deal with internal politics on behalf of the developers (effectively acting as a simplifying interface), and perform a lot of manual monitoring tasks which either couldn’t be or hadn't yet been automated. In this scenario, having specialised teams and a clear separation of concerns seemed to work well.
