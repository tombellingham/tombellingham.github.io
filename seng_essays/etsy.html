Would you trust a stranger to run your shop?

You’re about to finish your degree in Computing and your mom’s handmade soap hobby is gaining traction. Her friends have mentioned Amazon Handmade, Shopify, and other virtual storefront providers. She’s heard mixed signals about Etsy and wants your advice - should she set up shop with them? You’re tempted to pull a fast ‘let me google that for you’ but you instead dive into their engineering blog, Code as Craft, with your Holmesian detective cap and spyglass, to see if Etsy engineers are worth their salt.

Etsy is an international peer-to-peer e-commerce platform for items that are handmade, vintage, custom, or unique [1] and their stated mission is to “keep commerce human” [2]. Their services are available on the web and as separate mobile applications for buyers and sellers on Android and iOS. They began in 2005 and now have over 700 employees with 2M active sellers, 50M live products [3], and their gross annual merchandise in 2018 grew by about 20% to nearly $4B [4] with an all-time high quarterly revenue of $200M in Q4 [5].

Throughout their rollercoaster history of stock value drops [6], service outages [7] [8], huge layoffs [9], and fighting off counterfeit goods from their marketplace [10], the software engineering team at Etsy have been faithfully documenting the evolution of their technology and practice. We’re going to explore their software lifecycle, approach to dev and ops, and their recent transition to the cloud, evaluating their practice throughout to see if these can boost our confidence in their platform.

Software Lifecycle

Ideas & Design

New ideas are published on a company-wide wiki (Atlassian Confluence) where everyone can see and discuss them before they’re picked up or dismissed by a relevant team [11]. Most teams consist of 3-7 engineers plus a designer and a product manager but, surprisingly, all of them are expected to write some code. Thus, engineers and designers work closely together rather than having a product “handed to them for implementation” [11] which leads to faster iterations and fewer misunderstandings. For some features, a product team will form a prototype group of live Etsy sellers who give direct feedback and suggestions about ongoing changes. Etsy also run software engineering crash courses for everyone in the company, taking them from zero technical knowledge to understanding database sharding, CDNs, and making changes to the live website [12]. By demystifying tech for employees and working with end users, more collaborations and ideas emerge which ultimately streamline the delivery of meaningful customer-facing changes.

Development

Every programmer has a virtual machine running a personal version of Etsy.com filled with test data. These VM’s are hosted on an internal cloud system and can be provisioned at the press of a button [13] so new employees, testing environments, and production servers can be set up in minutes [14]. The VM’s are designed to mirror the production environment as closely as possible (maintained with Chef), which inhibits bugs from creeping in which might only emerge in production [13].

There is one GitHub repository for each application-OS pair [15]. Instead of using feature branches, all changes are pushed straight to master (á la continuous integration) and feature flags are used with config files to control the activation of features in different environments or according to developer preferences [16]. This means that releasing or removing features is as easy as pushing a one-line change to a config file [17]. In our class discussion on 5/2/19, the consensus was that feature branches were a bad idea because of having to resolve merge conflicts; ambiguity about when to branch new features, which changes should go on which branch, and when to merge; and the added complexity and cost of testing code across many branches. In this regard Etsy have found that feature flags are an elegant solution which also “reinforce the benefit of small changes and continuous delivery.” [17]

Prior to pushing changes, developers “rely heavily on code reviews” [18] and automated testing of patches on a ‘try’ server which can be executed before committing to master [19]. In our class on 5/2/19, Giovanni Asproni said that a team should be clear about what code reviews are for because pair programming is a more effective way to detect and prevent bugs and there are many other ways to share knowledge. A former senior software engineer at Etsy argued that code reviews with multiple co-workers can improve readability, discourage ‘clever’ solutions in favour of simpler ones inspired by the insights of other team members, and increase communal awareness [20]. Thus, code reviews at Etsy are less about detecting bugs and more about devising the best and most readable solutions for each problem and keeping each team tightly integrated. [20]

Continuous Integration

With tens of thousands of automated tests [21] and quick, reliable, one-button staging and deployment [22], Etsy deliver web code changes around 50 times per day [15] and mobile app updates as fast as app stores can review them. Since “Mobile apps live and die by their ratings in an App Store” [23], continuous integration helps Etsy to “try things out, fail, and revert without negatively affecting [their] users.” [17]

Building

A Jenkins CI server will make web builds whenever changes are pushed and notify developers about any issues via IRC and e-mail [18], so build problems can be resolved in minutes. The mobile apps are also built on push, but a separate version is also built daily which is available to all employees and which they are encouraged to use for testing feedback [18].

Testing

For the web code, Etsy divide thousands of PHPUnit tests into annotated subsets which can be executed concurrently on a cluster of Jenkins machines, so developers can run the most important tests in about 11 minutes. Shaving time on testing is crucial because it can easily become a bottleneck during continuous delivery - changes can only be safely released as fast as tests can be completed. Flaky tests are put in an annotated group and can be optionally executed during development but are otherwise ignored during regular deployments.

For mobile applications, Etsy use Calabash for cross-platform GUI acceptance tests and Cucumber for automated behaviour driven development - together these tools enable the QA team to create their own functional tests for iOS and Android apps [18]. These tests perform business-critical tasks like signing in, searching for a product, and making a purchase by simulating on-screen gestures and actions (which looks cool!). These tests are executed whenever new code is pushed, both on simulated devices in Etsy’s data centre and on Appthwack’s [24] fleet of hundreds of physical devices with different OS versions. They also run nightly regression tests on an even broader range of devices [18]. Together these tests make it highly unlikely for a change to cause drastic damage and also easy to detect and triage problems that do occur. It is important for Etsy to test changes against multiple versions of each app and OS because mobile users aren’t forced to update, so there are many live combinations which they don’t want to disrupt.

Deploying

Etsy argue that having a solid deployment pipeline is essential even without CI because one day you’ll want to safely ‘fast-lane’ an emergency change [22]. They built Deployinator (open source) - a web-based tool linked to their IRC channels, e-mail, and their logging and monitoring tools - which allows 1 person to deploy code in under 2 minutes instead of taking 3 developers and an operations engineer an hour [22]. As was recommended in our class discussion on 5/2/19, this pipeline allows hotfixes to skip code reviews and unnecessary tests.

New engineers are expected to push code to production on their first day [13]. This is made possible by infrastructure and tooling: getting a newly provisioned dev VM and pushing changes through staging to production are one-button actions, but production is guarded by attentive peers and a battery of automated tests. This baptism into the release cycle quickly squashes deployment fears and boosts developer productivity. Etsy believe that automated tools like Deployinator are what enabled them to grow their engineering team quickly [22].

Etsy automated away the need for a ‘release manager’ for mobile apps, which was a full time job, by developing Ship, which manages the state and schedule of all releases while keeping track of who changed what and when and sending targeted and detailed messages to the right people with instructions, updates, and feedback throughout the release process [15]. Ship even automates submitting apps to Google Play and iTunes Connect, releasing updates to the public (including gradual roll-out on Android), and collecting crash logs. In the previous workflow, everything above was done manually, which was tedious, error-prone, and not transparent to the developers - “engineers felt more like they were in the cargo hold, rather than in the cockpit,” [15] but now “Engineers control when a release goes to prod. Engineers decide if we’re ready to branch. Engineers hit the buttons.” [15]

Operations

Team Topology

According to Etsy’s former CTO, John Allspaw, they take a “you build it, you own it” approach to operations for product teams [25], however there is a dedicated ops engineering team who provide tooling to make this possible [14], which is cross between ‘Type 1’ and ‘Type 3’ DevOps team topologies [26] - every team is responsible for their own operations, but they’re aided by domain specialists who create reusable tooling (much like Netflix [27]). Etsy run internal ‘OpsSchool’ lectures to educate employees about ops [14] which help to ensure product engineers know what they’re doing. A few key ingredients are needed for ‘operate what you build’ to work: strong technical leadership, sufficient staff, ongoing training, and great tooling - all of which Etsy provide.

Post-Mortems

Etsy are well-known for practicing blameless post-mortems [28] [29] [30], where engineers are encouraged to be honest about their mistakes [31] and even celebrated for creating the most surprising unintended consequences [20] - this approach increases learning and reduces fear as engineers are “not only willing to be held accountable, they are also enthusiastic in helping the rest of the company avoid the same error in the future. They are, after all, the most expert in their own error.” [30] Etsy have published a debriefing facilitation guide to help other organisations adopt a similar practice [32].

Monitoring

The philosophy is simple: measure everything [33]. In 2010 Etsy were collecting 30,000 metrics [34] across their network, machines, and applications. With their open-source metric aggregation tool, StatsD, tracking something new is as easy as firing a UDP message with a unique metric identifier to a StatsD daemon. In our class discussion on 19/2/19, Sarah Wells said it needs to be easy for developers to add monitoring and logging, and Etsy write that “we enable engineers to track what they need to track, at the drop of a hat, without requiring time-sucking configuration changes or complicated processes” and stored metrics are very easy to “view, share, and brag about.” [33]

With many releases happening daily, Etsy track all changes to production servers so that they can be correlated with changes in metrics [35]. This makes it relatively easy to disambiguate human-induced changes in metric behaviour from other causes like hardware failures or broken 3rd-party APIs [35], which is “essential for teams who practice Continuous Deployment” [35] because any problematic code changes that creep through the review and testing process can be detected and resolved quickly.

Embracing the Cloud

Advantages

In our class discussion on 26/2/19, Paul Johnston expressed that he is worried when a business is not ‘moving through the continuum’ from on premise operations to more cloud based services. Etsy had been hosting their own servers since they began in 2005; in 2015 one operations engineer, Jon Cowie, said Etsy didn’t need to use cloud services because “the company is able to accurately predict how demand for its site’s services are likely to change throughout the year.” [36] However, after announcing a move to Google Cloud in 2018 [37] the current CTO, Mike Fisher, argued that on-premise hosting was “the right decision at the time” but cloud services have changed dramatically and “this shift allows us to spend less time maintaining our own infrastructure and more time on strategic features and services that make the Etsy marketplace great.” [38]

Etsy want to be powered by 100% renewable electricity by 2020, and the cloud transition is helping them to do that - they can spin up and spin down “hundreds of servers in minutes” [39] without months of planning for and powering machines that aren’t always used.

Challenges

Choosing a cloud provider was a complicated process; Etsy engineers hosted 25 architectural review sessions with many senior engineers over 5 months, frequently producing 30+ page documents for review [38]. To decide which system components to migrate and in what order, they had to aggregate detailed knowledge from many teams and create a graph of dependencies, which involved “teams of engineers huddled around whiteboards.” [38]

To mitigate the uncertainty of migration, they moved some small services to different cloud providers to get a sense of how difficult that would be. They also had many meetings with Google engineers, including “full day deep dives into various topics” [38], and discussions with other companies who had also migrated.

Since the cloud transition could have wide-ranging impact on many teams, it was important to involve everyone in selecting a provider, so they used a decision matrix to assimilate thousands of data points from stakeholders, cloud vendors, and engineering teams. The matrix included functional requirements (cost, ease of use, security, locations, etc…) and individual team requirements weighted by how much they contributed to the main goals.

Their aggressive 2-year migration plan is well underway, with the main site and mobile applications already on GCP, which was achieved “quickly and with minimal disruption to our buyers and sellers.” [39]

Conclusion

Many craftspeople depend on Etsy for all or part of their income, which is why it’s good that the engineers can say “Making the site fast and resilient to failure isn’t a side project, it’s core to what we’re doing.” [34]

With tight engineering teams, solid CI and automated testing, and streamlined deployment; everything monitored, strong technical leadership, and investment in great tooling; openness with their technology and practice, a culture of learning and blameless post-mortems, and a decisive embrace of cloud computing, engineering at Etsy is eminently commendable.

You probably wouldn’t recommend Etsy to your mom just because you like their industrial software engineering practice, but knowing of their intelligent, honest, and scalable approach to software - their Code as Craft - you may be more willing to do so.

“In a time of increasing automation, it’s our mission to keep human connection at the heart of commerce.” - Etsy [2]
